01 — Definition

What is Elasticity of Supply?

Elasticity of Supply (Es) measures the degree of responsiveness of quantity supplied to a change in the price of a commodity, while keeping all other factors constant.

In simpler terms: if the price of wheat rises by 10%, by how much (in percentage) will farmers increase the quantity supplied? That responsiveness is captured by the elasticity of supply.

Es = (% Change in Quantity Supplied) ÷ (% Change in Price)

The elasticity of supply is always positive because price and quantity supplied move in the same direction (law of supply). When price rises, suppliers are willing to supply more; when price falls, they supply less.

02 — Measurement

Formula & Calculation

Proportionate (Percentage) Method

This is the most important method for ISC examinations:

Es = (ΔQs / Qs) ÷ (ΔP / P)
= (ΔQs × P) / (ΔP × Qs)

Where: ΔQs = Change in Quantity Supplied  |  Qs = Original Quantity  |  ΔP = Change in Price  |  P = Original Price

Geometric Method (from Graph)

For a supply curve drawn on a graph, if the supply curve is a straight line:

Es = Lower segment / Upper segment
(when measuring from the price axis)
OR: Es = Intercept on X-axis / Intercept on Y-axis

05 — Determinants

Factors Determining Elasticity of Supply

⏱️
Time Period

In the market period, supply is perfectly inelastic (fixed). In the short run, it is less elastic. In the long run, supply is more elastic as producers can adjust all inputs. Longer time = more elastic supply.

🏭
Nature of the Commodity

Agricultural goods (perishable) have inelastic supply — output cannot be changed quickly. Industrial goods have elastic supply because production can be increased rapidly with more inputs.

💰
Cost of Production

If increasing output leads to rapidly rising costs, supply is inelastic. If costs rise slowly with output (constant or decreasing cost industries), supply is more elastic.

🔄
Mobility of Factors

Greater mobility of factors of production (labour, capital) between industries makes supply more elastic, as producers can shift resources in response to price changes.

📦
Availability of Stocks

If a commodity can be stored and stocks are available, supply is more elastic. Goods that cannot be stored (e.g., fresh fish) have inelastic supply.

🌍
Technique of Production

Goods produced with simple techniques respond quickly to price signals (elastic). Goods requiring complex, capital-intensive production have inelastic supply.
